This appendix documents the synthetic control construction methodology considered during the thesis design. This follows the synthetic-control method used in comparative case studies and later guidance on feasibility and data needs \cite{abadie2010synthetic, abadie2021synthetic}. It summarizes the control basket, weight construction, hold-out validation, and the reason CAR (Cumulative Abnormal Returns) was not retained as the main outcome.

Table~\ref{tab:app-control-basket} lists the default macro-market control basket. It consisted of eight major cryptocurrency assets selected to represent broad market movements that were not tied to one specific micro-cap pump.

This construction also required a weighting rule. The counterfactual was constructed as a weighted average of control-asset returns using a CLS (Constrained Least Squares) rule over pre-reveal validation windows, following the synthetic-control logic of \cite{abadie2010synthetic}.

To limit data contamination and overfitting, testing used trailing periods anchored before the public reveal at $T_0$.
\begin{itemize}
    \item Training Window. $[T_0 - 84\,\mathrm{h}, T_0 - 36\,\mathrm{h})$ (used to choose the CLS weights)
    \item Hold-Out Validation Data. Aligned pre-reveal price data ending at $T_0 - 12\,\mathrm{h}$ (used for out-of-sample $R^2$ validation)
\end{itemize}

A 12-hour gap before the public reveal at $T_0$ reduced the risk that early coordination activity entered the baseline period.

The weight vector $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ represented a long-only portfolio of control assets through standard weight constraints.

\begin{equation}
    \min_{\boldsymbol{\omega}} \| \mathbf{y} - \mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\omega} \|_2^2
    \quad \text{subject to} \quad
    \sum_{k} \omega_k = 1, \quad \boldsymbol{\omega} \geq 0
\end{equation}

where $\mathbf{y}$ is the vector of returns for the target coin during the training window, $\mathbf{X}$ is the matrix of returns for the macro-market control basket, and $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ is the vector of weights assigned to each control asset. The index $k$ refers to an asset in the control basket, and $\|\cdot\|_2^2$ denotes the squared Euclidean norm. The constraints ensure the synthetic control is a fully invested ($\sum \omega_k = 1$) and long-only ($\boldsymbol{\omega} \geq 0$) portfolio.

The optimization used logarithmic returns because absolute prices are not stationary over time. This approach prevented small discrepancies in price trends from causing the synthetic control and target coin paths to drift apart during multi-day evaluations.

The validation step then evaluated whether these controls tracked target coins before the reveal. Table~\ref{tab:app-holdout-r2-validation} reports the out-of-sample validation results. These diagnostics use the broader synthetic-control validation set, not the final 2,917-event complete-case causal sample. The tests showed that micro-cap markets often move independently from the broader economy.

The near-zero median hold-out fit indicates that coins targeted by pump-and-dump groups are generally not driven by typical market-wide trends.

This weak tracking result also explains why CAR was not retained as the main outcome.

Initially, this study used CAR to try and isolate the coin's performance from broader market movements.
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{CAR}_{i,\Delta} = r_{\text{target},i}^{[0,\Delta]} - r_{\text{control},i}^{[0,\Delta]}
\label{eq:car-original}
\end{equation}
where $r_{\text{target},i}^{[0,\Delta]}$ is the actual log-return and $r_{\text{control},i}^{[0,\Delta]}$ is the synthetic estimate of the return that would have occurred without the pump. Here, $i$ indexes the event and $\Delta$ is the post-reveal horizon.

However, this method did not work well for our sample. As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:app-r2-distribution}, the median $R^2$ between synthetic controls and actual prices was close to zero, and the mean remained low. The model failed to predict prices accurately for the micro-cap and small-cap coins that pump-and-dump groups typically target.

The synthetic controls failed to track the target coins, so using CAR actually made the results less reliable. Instead of removing market noise, subtracting an unrelated control variable just added more variance to the data.
\begin{equation}
\operatorname{Var}(\mathrm{CAR}) = \operatorname{Var}(r_{\text{target}}) + \operatorname{Var}(r_{\text{control}}) - 2\operatorname{Cov}(r_{\text{target}}, r_{\text{control}})
\end{equation}
For readability, the event and horizon indices are omitted in this equation. Here, $\operatorname{Var}$ denotes variance and $\operatorname{Cov}$ denotes covariance. When the two variables are completely unrelated (zero covariance), CAR results in higher variance than the original returns. This extra noise can hide the true statistical effects of the pump.

If we had filtered the sample to only include cases where the synthetic control worked (e.g., $R^2 > 0.25$), we would have lost over 75\% of our data. More importantly, this would have removed almost all low-liquidity and micro-cap coins. This would have shifted our focus entirely to large-cap assets that are rarely affected by these groups. For these reasons, it was decided not to use CAR. Instead, the standard logarithmic returns were used and market conditions were handled directly within the Double Machine Learning models.

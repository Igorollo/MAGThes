Coordinated cryptocurrency pumps offer a clear way to study how public messages can move prices in a short time window. In these schemes, a pump signal asks many traders to buy the same asset at almost the same time. If enough traders react quickly, the price may rise for a short period. Early buyers or insiders can then sell to later buyers. The main question is which parts of the signal help create this short-term price reaction.

This setting is important because the signal is visible, the timing is clear, and the market reaction can be measured soon after the reveal. Telegram pump groups often use countdowns, urgent wording, and direct trading instructions. These messages do not need to contain new fundamental information. Their main role is to coordinate attention and trading pressure around the same moment. For this reason, the thesis treats Telegram messages as social coordination signals rather than as normal news.

Previous research has already shown that cryptocurrency pump-and-dump schemes can be detected and described. This thesis builds on that work, but it asks a narrower causal question. It studies whether specific signal characteristics, especially urgency, affect immediate returns after the target coin is revealed. It also asks a separate predictive question: whether observable market and signal features can help classify which pumps will end with a positive short-term return.

\section{Cryptocurrency Market Manipulation and Pump-and-Dump Schemes}
\label{sec:crypto-manipulation-literature}

Market manipulation is not a new problem. In classic theory, prices can be moved when traders create actions or signals that other market participants interpret as useful information \cite{allen1992stock}. The manipulator does not always need better information about the true value of the asset. It can be enough to create buying pressure or to make other traders believe that demand is increasing. A pump-and-dump scheme follows this logic because price pressure and information pressure work together.

Empirical work on traditional stock markets shows that manipulation often appears in markets where some traders have better access, better timing, or more control over information flows. Aggarwal and Wu show that stock manipulation can involve trading that creates artificial price movements, followed by profitable selling by the manipulator \cite{aggarwal2006stock}. Khwaja and Mian study manipulation in an emerging stock market and show how weak controls and powerful intermediaries can make price manipulation easier \cite{khwaja2005unchecked}. These studies matter for cryptocurrency markets because many cryptoassets share some features of less mature markets, such as limited oversight, uneven information, and weaker investor protection.

Cryptocurrency markets add several conditions that can make manipulation easier to organize. Trading is split across many exchanges, and prices can differ across venues \cite{makarov2020trading}. Many assets also have high volatility and unstable trading activity \cite{liu2021risks}. Some tokens trade with very thin order flow, so a modest wave of buying can move the price more than it would in a deep market. These features do not mean that every crypto price movement is manipulation. They mean that coordinated buying can have a visible effect when market depth is limited.

These same features also make the empirical problem harder. A sharp price rise after a signal may look like manipulation, but crypto prices can also move sharply without any organized event. Thin trading, exchange differences, and normal volatility can all create short bursts in returns. For that reason, the literature on crypto manipulation is useful not only because it documents pumps, but also because it shows why simple before-and-after price changes need careful interpretation. The later empirical design follows this concern by keeping the focus on short windows and by controlling for pre-reveal market conditions.

Price manipulation has also been documented inside cryptocurrency markets. Gandal and coauthors show that suspicious trading activity played an important role in early Bitcoin price movements \cite{gandal2018price}. Later studies focus more directly on pump-and-dump schemes. Kamps and Kleinberg define and detect crypto pumps, while Victor and Hagemann measure their scale and detectability \cite{kamps2018pump, victor2019cryptocurrency}. Xu and Livshits describe the structure of pump groups and show how public signals can be linked to market reactions \cite{xu2019anatomy}. Li, Shin, and Wang also study cryptocurrency pump-and-dump schemes and show that they are not isolated events \cite{li2021cryptocurrency}.

A pump-and-dump scheme depends on timing. Organizers first choose a target asset that can attract buyers but may still be cheap to move. Before the reveal, the group may build attention through reminders, countdowns, or promotional messages. At the reveal, the target coin is announced and members are asked to buy. If buying arrives quickly, the price may rise in the first minutes. When early buyers sell into this demand, late buyers can be left with losses after the price falls back.

The literature also shows that pump participation is not always based on a simple mistake. Some participants may know that the event is risky, but they still hope to buy early and sell before others. Dhawan and Putnins describe this problem as a new form of pump-and-dump manipulation in cryptocurrency markets, where open participation can continue even when the average participant faces poor odds \cite{dhawan2023new}. This point is important for the thesis because it shifts attention away from false information alone. In many Telegram pumps, the key issue is fast coordination around a known trading game.

Market size is central to this setting. A large asset can usually absorb more orders before the price changes sharply. A smaller asset, especially one with weak trading depth, can react more strongly to the same amount of buying. The idea is linked to illiquidity: when a market is less liquid, a given trade has a larger price effect \cite{amihud2002illiquidity}. Market capitalization is not the same as liquidity, and it does not measure the order book directly. Still, it is a useful proxy for how resistant an asset may be to coordinated demand.

This market-capitalization logic helps connect the literature to the later empirical design. Pump organizers need assets that are visible enough to trade but small enough to move. Very large coins may be too liquid for a Telegram signal to create a strong price jump. Extremely small coins may also be difficult because they may have too little trading activity or poor data coverage. For the empirical analysis, the variable Market Capitalization is therefore treated as a key source of variation in manipulation effectiveness, while the detailed sample construction and market-capitalization strata are left to the later chapters.

\section{Telegram Signals and Social Coordination}
\label{sec:telegram-social-coordination}

Pump-and-dump schemes depend on communication. In stock markets, rumors and tips could spread through personal networks, newsletters, or message boards. Van Bommel shows how rumors can affect market behavior when traders react before information is fully verified \cite{vanbommel2003rumors}. Sabherwal, Sarkar, and Zhang show that Internet stock message boards can influence trading in heavily discussed stocks even when there is no clear fundamental news \cite{sabherwal2011internet}. These studies help explain why public messages can matter even when they do not change the true value of an asset.

Telegram changes the speed and structure of this communication. A group administrator can send one message to many users at the same time. Members can wait for a target reveal, prepare funds on an exchange, and react within minutes. The signal is therefore not only information about which coin to buy. It is also a timing device. It tells participants when to act and helps turn many separate traders into a temporary coordinated group.

A Telegram pump signal also differs from ordinary investment discussion. It is usually short, direct, and tied to an expected action. The message often does not ask members to form a long-term view about the asset. Instead, it asks them to react quickly before other traders do. This makes the language of the signal important. A message that is urgent, clear, and close to the reveal time may create a faster response than a message that is calm, vague, or spread out over a longer period.

Research on cryptocurrency manipulation has already shown that social media and messaging platforms are part of the pump process. Mirtaheri and coauthors study cryptocurrency manipulation in social media and show that online signals can be linked to suspicious market behavior \cite{mirtaheri2021identifying}. Xu and Livshits, Li, Shin, and Wang, and La Morgia and coauthors also show that Telegram or similar channels are used to coordinate pump-and-dump activity \cite{xu2019anatomy, li2021cryptocurrency, lamorgia2023doge}. This literature supports the idea that message content is not only background noise. It is part of how the event is organized.

The central moment in the event is the public reveal of the target coin. This moment is denoted as $T_0$. Before $T_0$, a channel may send countdowns, reminders, and messages that build attention. At $T_0$, the coin is revealed and traders can act on the signal. The signal and the price response can then be connected inside a short window, while information after the reveal can be kept separate from information available before it.

The thesis focuses on message characteristics that describe the timing, style, and content of the signal. Hype Intensity describes whether preparatory messages are concentrated close to the reveal time. If many messages arrive shortly before $T_0$, attention may be compressed into a narrow window. This can make the first minutes after the reveal more important because more traders may be watching at the same time.

Urgency Index describes the visible style of the main signal message. It is measured through the share of uppercase characters, so it is only a proxy. It does not measure fear, excitement, or trader psychology directly. It shows whether the message looks more forceful and immediate. In pump groups, this may matter because a message that looks like an immediate command can reduce hesitation.

Instruction Density describes how much instruction the main signal contains. Some pump messages only name the coin. Others include the exchange, trading pair, target price, holding advice, or selling cues. More instruction content can make the desired action clearer, but it can also make the signal more complex. Hype Volume, by contrast, measures the amount of preparatory communication. It can help describe and predict a session, but it is not part of the final causal treatment vector. The causal analysis focuses on Hype Intensity, Urgency Index, and Instruction Density.

These variables do not assume that every participant believes the signal or that the signal contains real news. The more important idea is that message style can coordinate action. A countdown can gather attention. An urgent reveal can shorten the time between reading and trading. A clear instruction can lower the effort needed to place an order. Together, these features help explain why Telegram signals may matter for short-term returns even when they do not carry fundamental information.

\section{From Detection to Causal Measurement}
\label{sec:detection-to-causal-measurement}

Much existing research studies how to detect pump schemes. Researchers ask whether a suspicious price pattern can be found, whether an event can be identified, or whether the target coin can be predicted before the reveal \cite{kamps2018pump, victor2019cryptocurrency, lamorgia2020pump, hu2023sequence, fu2025perseus}. This work is valuable because it shows that pump schemes can be observed and measured. It also gives tools for finding events in large amounts of market and message data.

Detection, however, is not the same as causal measurement. A detection model may say that an event looks like a pump because price and volume changed sharply. A target prediction model may say which coin is likely to be announced. These tasks do not answer whether a specific signal feature, such as urgency, caused a stronger return after the reveal. For that question, the analysis must separate the signal feature from other market conditions that also affect the price.

This separation is difficult because pump signals are not random. Organizers may choose coins that are already volatile, thinly traded, or showing unusual activity before the event. They may also write stronger messages when they expect the coin to move easily. If a coin rises after an urgent message, the rise may come from the message, but it may also come from the coin's pre-existing market state. Without adjustment, the analysis could mistake normal market vulnerability for a direct signal effect.

The event-level view also matters here. The unit of interest is not every Telegram message on its own. It is the pump session around the reveal. This keeps the analysis close to the way the scheme is organized: messages build attention, the target coin is revealed, and the market reacts. It also helps avoid mixing pre-reveal signals with post-reveal reactions. The causal question is therefore framed around information available before or at $T_0$, not around messages sent after traders have already seen the target coin.

The causal framing uses a few basic ideas. A treatment is the feature whose effect is being studied. Here, the final treatments are Hype Intensity, Urgency Index, and Instruction Density. The outcome is the short-term price response after the reveal, measured as the 10-minute logarithmic return from $T_0$ to $T_0+10$. Controls, also called confounders, are variables that may affect both the treatment and the outcome. They include pre-reveal market and asset conditions, together with channel fixed effects that account for stable differences between Telegram channels.

A causal effect is the change in the outcome that can be linked to a treatment after accounting for these background factors. The idea follows the potential outcomes view of causality \cite{rubin1974estimating}. For the same event, one can think about the observed return after the actual signal and the return that would have happened under a different signal style. Only one of these paths is observed. The other path is a counterfactual. The empirical task is to make the comparison as credible as possible with observational data \cite{angrist2009mostly}.

The short event window helps, but it does not solve the whole problem. A 10-minute return keeps the analysis close to the signal and limits the influence of later market news. Still, even a short return can be affected by pre-reveal volatility, liquidity, market capitalization, and channel behavior. This is why the later empirical design controls for information available before the reveal. The aim is not to remove all uncertainty. The aim is to compare events that are more similar in their pre-event conditions.

Prediction answers a different question. A predictive model asks whether available features can classify whether the pump will end with a positive 10-minute return. Such a model can use variables that are useful for forecasting, even if they are not causal drivers. For example, a feature may help predict success because it reflects the strategy of a strong organizer. That does not mean the feature itself causes the price increase. The thesis therefore keeps causal effect estimation and prediction as related but separate tasks.

\section{Theoretical Expectations and Hypotheses}
\label{sec:theoretical-expectations-hypotheses}

The hypotheses follow from three linked ideas. The message may affect how fast traders react. The market may affect how strongly the price moves. The same information may also help predict whether a pump ends with a positive return. These ideas are connected, but they are not identical.

The first expectation follows from the coordination role of Telegram messages. Pump organizers want many traders to act quickly after the target coin is revealed. Urgent wording can support this goal because it makes the signal look immediate. A signal written in a forceful style may reduce waiting, checking, or hesitation among participants. If this creates faster buying pressure, the effect should appear soon after $T_0$, not hours later.

\textbf{H1:} More urgent Telegram pump signals are expected to have a direct positive effect on short-term cryptocurrency returns after the coin reveal, especially in lower-cap market segments where prices are easier to move.

The expectation is cautious. Urgency may speed up coordinated buying, but it may not work in every market. If it matters, the response should be visible in the immediate minutes after the reveal, before the pump loses attention or late sellers enter the market.

The second expectation concerns the asset. The same message should not have the same effect for every coin. A large and active market can absorb more orders. A smaller and thinner market can move more sharply when many traders buy at once. This is why the manipulation literature and the illiquidity literature both point toward market size and market depth as important conditions \cite{amihud2002illiquidity, dhawan2023new}.

\textbf{H2:} Short-term pump returns are expected to be lower as Market Capitalization increases, with the strongest impacts expected in lower-cap and especially small-cap assets where liquidity is limited but trading is still active.

This expectation does not say that every low-cap asset is easy to manipulate. Some very small assets may have too little trading activity, and some larger assets may still react during special market conditions. The expected pattern is broader: as the target asset becomes larger and more resistant to order pressure, the same coordinated signal should have a weaker short-term effect.

The third expectation concerns prediction. If pump success depends on signal style and pre-event market conditions, then those features should contain useful information before the outcome is known. The predictive task does not ask whether a feature causes success. It asks whether the observed information is enough to classify events into successful and unsuccessful cases. In this thesis, success means a positive 10-minute logarithmic return after the reveal.

\textbf{H3:} Observable market patterns before the event, combined with Telegram signal characteristics, contain information that can help predict whether the manipulation will succeed.

H3 is therefore separate from H1 and H2. H1 and H2 concern causal effects and how those effects change across market size. H3 concerns forecasting. A model may predict success well because it combines many weak signals, including market thinness, coin age, message style, and pre-event anomalies. This can be useful even when the model does not give a causal explanation for each feature.

\section{Causal and Predictive Framework}
\label{sec:causal-predictive-framework}

The empirical framework uses causal inference and machine learning together. This combination is needed because the setting is noisy. Short-term crypto returns can react to many pre-event conditions, and these relationships may not be linear. At the same time, a pure prediction model would not answer the main causal question. The thesis therefore uses machine learning to help adjust for background patterns, while keeping the final causal question focused on the signal features.

Double Machine Learning is used for this purpose \cite{chernozhukov2018double}. In plain terms, DML uses machine learning to account for patterns in the pre-reveal controls. It then estimates the relationship that remains between the signal feature and the return. This helps reduce bias from observed confounders while keeping the estimated effect interpretable. Chapter~\ref{ch:methodology} presents the formal model and the implementation details.

The analysis also studies whether effects differ across assets. An average effect can hide important differences. A signal may work in small-cap assets but not in larger assets. Causal Forests are used to study this type of treatment-effect heterogeneity \cite{breiman2001random, athey2019generalized}. Their role is to show how the estimated effect changes across the Market Capitalization distribution, without forcing all events to have the same response.

The predictive part uses XGBoost \cite{xgboost2016}. XGBoost is a tree-based machine learning method built for prediction. Its role in the thesis is not to estimate a causal effect. It tests whether pre-event market variables and Telegram signal features can classify pump success out of sample. This matters because a market pattern can be useful for prediction even when it is not a direct cause of the return.

The separation between causal and predictive work matters for the interpretation of the later chapters. The causal analysis asks whether a signal characteristic changes the 10-minute return after adjustment for confounders. The predictive analysis asks whether available information can help identify events that will end with a positive return. These questions use related variables, but they do not have the same meaning.

Synthetic control methods were also considered because they can create a comparison path for an observed asset \cite{abadie2010synthetic, abadie2021synthetic}. In principle, such a path could help measure abnormal returns. In this thesis, synthetic control is not the final outcome design. The final causal outcome is the 10-minute logarithmic return from $T_0$ to $T_0+10$. Diagnostics from the synthetic-control exercise are kept only as auxiliary predictive features, while the detailed discussion is left to Appendix~\ref{app:synthetic-control}.

\section{Contribution and Thesis Structure}
\label{sec:chapter-one-contribution}

The thesis contributes to the study of cryptocurrency market manipulation in three main ways. First, it links social coordination signals with short-term market reactions in an event setting. This makes it possible to study the message and the price response as parts of the same process. Second, it estimates the direct effect of selected signal characteristics instead of only detecting pump events. Third, it studies whether manipulation success differs by Market Capitalization and whether success can be predicted from information available before or at the reveal.

The chapter also frames Telegram pump messages as coordination tools. Their power may come from timing, urgency, and instruction, not from fundamental information about the asset. This view connects the literature on manipulation with the literature on online communication and short-horizon trading. It also explains why causal estimation and prediction are treated as separate tasks.

The remaining chapters develop this argument step by step. Chapter~\ref{ch:data-description} describes the data, event construction, market information, and feature engineering. Chapter~\ref{ch:methodology} presents the outcome definition, causal framework, heterogeneous-effect analysis, and predictive design. Chapter~\ref{ch:results} reports the empirical results for the hypotheses. The appendix discusses the synthetic-control exercise and explains why it is not used as the final outcome design.

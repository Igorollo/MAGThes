Coordinated cryptocurrency pumps provide a unique environment to study how public messages influence market prices within very short timeframes. In these schemes, a pump signal is broadcasted to a large number of traders, instructing them to buy a specific asset simultaneously. If enough participants react quickly, the sudden wave of demand can drive the price up for a brief period, allowing early buyers or insiders to sell at a profit to those who enter later. The central question for this research is identifying which specific elements of these signals are responsible for driving the short-term price reaction.

This unique experimental setting is particularly suitable for research because the signals are public, the timing is precise, and the market's response follows almost immediately after the reveal. Messaging platform pump groups typically use countdowns, forceful wording, and explicit trading instructions to organize their members. These messages often lack any new fundamental information about the asset; instead, their primary function is to coordinate attention and trading pressure at a single moment. Consequently, this thesis treats coordinated signals as tools for social coordination rather than as conventional financial news.

While previous research has already established that these schemes can be detected and described, this thesis takes a more focused causal approach. It investigates whether specific characteristics of a signal---such as its perceived urgency---directly impact returns in the minutes following a coin reveal. Alongside this causal inquiry, the study also addresses a predictive question: whether observable market conditions and signal features can help classify which events are likely to result in positive short-term returns.

\section{Cryptocurrency Market Manipulation and Pump-and-Dump Schemes}
\label{sec:crypto-manipulation-literature}

Market manipulation has been a persistent challenge in financial history. Theoretical models suggest that prices can be moved when traders take actions or broadcast signals that others interpret as valuable information \cite{allen1992stock}. Manipulators do not always require a deep understanding of an asset's intrinsic value. Instead, it often suffices to generate a surge in buying pressure or to lead other participants to believe that demand is rising. A pump-and-dump scheme follows this logic, relying on the combined effect of price pressure and informational signals to move the market.

Empirical studies of traditional stock markets show that manipulation is most common where there are significant gaps in information access, timing, or control. For instance, Aggarwal and Wu demonstrate that manipulation often involves trading activity that creates artificial price movements, followed by the manipulator's profitable exit \cite{aggarwal2006stock}. Similarly, research on emerging markets highlights how weak oversight and the presence of powerful intermediaries can make price manipulation easier to carry out \cite{khwaja2005unchecked}. These findings are highly relevant to cryptocurrency markets, which often share the characteristics of less mature financial environments, such as limited regulation, uneven information distribution, and weaker protection for investors.

The structure of cryptocurrency markets introduces additional factors that facilitate coordinated manipulation. Trading is fragmented across numerous exchanges, leading to price discrepancies between venues \cite{makarov2020trading}. Furthermore, many digital assets suffer from extreme volatility and inconsistent trading volume \cite{liu2021risks}. Some tokens trade in such "thin" markets that even a modest wave of buying can move their price much more than it would in a more liquid market. While these features do not mean that every sharp price change is the result of manipulation, they do suggest that coordinated buying can have a disproportionate impact when market depth is low.

These complexities also make empirical analysis more difficult. A sharp price spike following a signal might look like manipulation, but in the crypto world, such moves can also happen naturally due to thin trading or general volatility. For this reason, the existing literature is valuable not only for documenting these events but also for highlighting why simple price comparisons require careful handling. This study addresses these concerns by narrowing the focus to very short time windows and explicitly controlling for market conditions before the signal is released.

Manipulation is well-documented within cryptocurrency ecosystems. Early research by Gandal and others showed that suspicious trading patterns played a significant role in Bitcoin's early price history \cite{gandal2018price}. More recent work has focused specifically on organized pump-and-dump groups. Kamps and Kleinberg introduced methods for defining and detecting these events, while Victor and Hagemann measured their scale and frequency \cite{kamps2018pump, victor2019cryptocurrency}. Other studies have mapped the internal structure of Telegram groups and linked public signals to specific market reactions \cite{xu2019anatomy, li2021cryptocurrency}.

Successful pump-and-dump schemes rely heavily on timing. Typically, organizers select a target asset that is prominent enough to attract interest but small enough that its price can be easily moved. Before the actual reveal, the group builds anticipation through reminders and countdowns. When the target coin is finally announced, members are encouraged to buy immediately. If the response is fast enough, the price spikes within the first few minutes. As early buyers start to sell, those who entered later are often left holding assets that rapidly lose their value.

The literature also suggests that participating in a pump is not always the result of a simple misunderstanding. Many participants are aware of the risks but gamble on being able to exit before the crash. Dhawan and Putnins describe this as a modern evolution of manipulation, where open participation continues even when the average trader faces poor odds \cite{dhawan2023new}. This perspective is important for this thesis because it moves the focus away from "fake news" and toward the mechanics of fast coordination in a high-stakes environment.

Market size plays a fundamental role in this process. Larger assets can usually absorb significant buying volume without experiencing sharp price swings. In contrast, smaller assets with limited trading depth are much more sensitive to coordinated demand. This relationship is closely tied to the concept of illiquidity: in a thin market, every individual trade has a larger impact on the price \cite{amihud2002illiquidity}. While market capitalization is not a perfect measure of liquidity, it serves as a reliable proxy for how resistant an asset is likely to be to sudden, organized shifts in demand.

This logic connects the existing literature to the empirical design of this study. Organizers need coins that are visible yet "moveable." Very large coins are often too liquid for a coordinated group to influence significantly, while the smallest coins may lack the data coverage needed for analysis. Consequently, this thesis treats Market Capitalization as a primary factor in determining how effective a manipulation attempt will be, with a detailed analysis of different market-cap segments appearing in later chapters.

\section{Messaging Platform Signals and Social Coordination}
\label{sec:telegram-social-coordination}

Effective pump-and-dump schemes are fundamentally built on communication. In traditional markets, rumors and tips were typically spread through personal networks, newsletters, or early online message boards. Van Bommel highlights how such rumors can drive market behavior when traders act before information is fully verified \cite{vanbommel2003rumors}. Similarly, research on internet stock boards shows that social discussion can influence trading even in the absence of fundamental news \cite{sabherwal2011internet}. These studies provide a basis for understanding why public signals can have a profound impact even when they do not reflect an asset's real value.

Digital messaging platforms have transformed the speed and structure of this coordination by turning dispersed traders into a shared audience for the same signal. Telegram is especially relevant in the empirical setting studied here, but the theoretical point is broader: broadcast channels can synchronize attention, timing, and expectations without adding fundamental information. The platform-specific collection procedures are described in Chapter~\ref{ch:data-description}.

A coordinated pump signal is distinct from typical investment discussions; it is usually brief, direct, and focused on immediate action. Rather than encouraging a long-term view of an asset, it demands a rapid response to capture short-term gains before others do. This emphasis on speed makes the phrasing of the signal critical. A message that conveys urgency and clarity is likely to trigger a faster response than one that is vague or poorly timed.

Extensive research has already linked social media and messaging platforms to cryptocurrency manipulation. Studies have shown that online signals are often directly connected to suspicious market volatility \cite{mirtaheri2021identifying, xu2019anatomy}. Work by Li, Shin, and Wang, as well as La Morgia and colleagues, further shows that Telegram and similar channels are important venues for coordinating these activities \cite{li2021cryptocurrency, lamorgia2023doge}. This body of literature suggests that the content and delivery of a message are not merely background details, but are integral to the organization of the event itself.

The pivotal moment in any pump is the public reveal of the target coin, denoted as $T_0$. Conceptually, messages around this point matter through three broad coordination dimensions: how attention is concentrated before the reveal, how urgent the reveal appears, and how clearly the signal translates attention into action. Chapter~\ref{ch:data-description} provides the operational feature definitions, while Chapter~\ref{ch:methodology} specifies how those features enter the empirical models.

These coordination dimensions do not assume that every participant believes the signal or that the asset has changed in value. Instead, they acknowledge that the style and timing of a message can coordinate mass behavior. A countdown focuses attention and an urgent tone speeds up reaction times. Together, these mechanisms help explain why coordinated signals may drive short-term returns without relying on traditional fundamental analysis.

\section{From Detection to Causal Measurement}
\label{sec:detection-to-causal-measurement}

A significant body of research has focused on the detection of pump schemes, typically investigating whether suspicious price patterns can be identified or whether a target coin can be predicted before its official reveal \cite{kamps2018pump, victor2019cryptocurrency, lamorgia2020pump, hu2023sequence, fu2025perseus}. This work is essential because it demonstrates that these events are observable and provides the tools necessary to isolate them within vast datasets of market and message activity.

While detection is a critical first step, it is distinct from causal measurement. A detection model can identify a pump based on sharp shifts in price and volume, and a prediction model might estimate which coin is likely to be targeted. However, these methods do not explain whether specific signal characteristics---such as the urgency of the message---actually caused a stronger market reaction. To answer that question, the analysis must separate the influence of the signal from other market factors that independently affect prices.

Isolating these effects is challenging because pump signals are not issued at random. Organizers often select coins that are already volatile, thinly traded, or showing unusual activity before the event. Furthermore, they may craft more aggressive messages when they anticipate that a coin is particularly vulnerable to price movement. Without proper adjustment, it is easy to mistake a coin's pre-existing market state for the direct effect of a signal.

To address this, the study adopts an event-level perspective, focusing on the pump session rather than isolated messages. This approach aligns with the real-world organization of these schemes: preparatory communication builds attention, the target coin is revealed, and the market reacts. The empirical chapters formalize this event structure by separating information available before or at the reveal from information observed afterward.

The causal framework relies on a simple conceptual distinction. Signal characteristics are the candidate drivers of interest, the short-horizon price response is the outcome to be explained, and pre-existing market or organizer conditions are possible confounders. Chapter~\ref{ch:methodology} defines the exact treatment vector, outcome window, and control set.

This approach follows the potential outcomes view of causality \cite{rubin1974estimating}. In this context, we can imagine two possible paths for a single event: the observed return following the actual signal, and the return that would have occurred had the signal been delivered in a different style. Since only one path is ever observable, the empirical task is to construct a credible comparison through observational data \cite{angrist2009mostly}.

The choice of how short this observation window should be depends on how these schemes actually work. Past studies show that cryptocurrency pump-and-dumps happen extremely fast. Xu and Livshits \cite{xu2019anatomy}, for example, report that the price usually reaches its peak just a few minutes after the coin announcement.

This is why the thesis focuses on a 10-minute window. This specific post-signal period is also supported by finance literature. Li, Shin, and Wang \cite{li2021cryptocurrency} analyze market activity in 10-second intervals up to 600 seconds after a pump announcement and show that even after ten minutes, price, trading volume, and volatility remain significantly higher than before the announcement. Their empirical design also defines maximum returns within this same 10-minute window. Based on this, the thesis measures return at the 10-minute mark to capture the signal’s immediate market impact.

A natural alternative might be to look for the maximum price reached during a broader window, such as the first 20 minutes. Choosing a fixed 10-minute mark, however, provides a stronger basis for comparison. It standardizes the evaluation across events by measuring the same phase of the manipulation each time. This is more reliable than using a maximum price, which may reflect only a brief trade in a thin market rather than a broader market reaction. A fixed checkpoint also better reflects trading reality, since traders do not know in advance when the peak will occur.

The 10-minute window is also short enough to limit the influence of unrelated market factors that become more important over longer horizons \cite{li2021cryptocurrency, lamorgia2023doge}. Looking at hourly or daily returns would be less appropriate, because pump-and-dumps do not reflect changes in fundamental value. Once the scheme ends, the targeted coin typically experiences a sharp price drop as the artificial inflation collapses \cite{kamps2018pump}. Staying within the first 10 minutes therefore helps isolate the effect of the coordinated signal itself.

Even though the 10-minute window reduces later market noise, it does not fully remove the influence of what was happening to the coin before the pump began. That is why the study still needs to control for the market conditions just before the signal was posted. The aim is not to eliminate all randomness, but to compare events that started from similar conditions.

Predictive modeling serves a different role. Instead of asking what caused the outcome, it asks whether the available information can accurately predict if a pump will lead to a positive return. A model like this can use variables that are useful for prediction even if they are not the actual drivers of the market reaction, such as the strategy used by a specific organizer. For this reason, the thesis treats causal analysis and prediction as two separate tasks, so that each is examined with the method that fits it best.

\section{Theoretical Expectations and Hypotheses}
\label{sec:theoretical-expectations-hypotheses}

The hypotheses for this study are derived from three interconnected theoretical premises. They concern how the message influences trader behavior, how market conditions constrain price movements, and how these factors combine to make success predictable.

The first hypothesis focuses on how coordinated messages facilitate group action. Since organizers aim to trigger immediate buying, they often use forceful and urgent wording to minimize hesitation among participants. If such messaging successfully speeds up the response, its impact should be most visible shortly after the reveal.

\textbf{H1:} More urgent pump signals are expected to have a direct positive effect on short-term cryptocurrency returns after the coin reveal, especially in lower-cap market segments where prices are easier to move.

It is important to note that urgency may not be effective in every market environment. If a signal does have a measurable impact, that response is expected to occur rapidly, before the initial momentum fades or sellers begin to exit their positions.

The second hypothesis focuses on the characteristics of the targeted asset. The same signal is unlikely to produce identical results across different coins. While a large, liquid market can absorb significant trading volume with minimal price disruption, a thin market is much more sensitive. This logic is well-supported by literature on market manipulation and illiquidity \cite{amihud2002illiquidity, dhawan2023new}.

\textbf{H2:} Short-term pump returns are expected to be lower as Market Capitalization increases, with the strongest impacts expected in lower-cap and especially small-cap assets where liquidity is limited but trading is still active.

This does not imply that all low-cap assets are easily manipulated; rather, it suggests a broader trend where an asset's resistance to organized demand increases alongside its market size.

The final hypothesis addresses predictability. If the success of a pump depends on a combination of signal style and pre-event market states, then those variables should contain meaningful information before the outcome is realized.

\textbf{H3:} Observable market patterns before the event, combined with signal characteristics, contain information that can help predict whether the manipulation will succeed.

While H1 and H2 focus on isolating causal drivers, H3 is concerned with forecasting. A model might predict success by combining many subtle signals, such as coin age or pre-event anomalies, even if it does not provide a direct causal explanation for each individual feature.

\section{Causal and Predictive Framework}
\label{sec:causal-predictive-framework}

To address these complexities, the empirical framework combines causal estimation with predictive modeling. The causal component asks whether signal characteristics are associated with short-horizon returns after accounting for relevant background conditions. The predictive component asks whether information available at the reveal can classify which events are likely to end successfully. Chapter~\ref{ch:methodology} presents the formal estimators and validation choices, while Appendix~\ref{app:synthetic-control} discusses the synthetic-control exercise considered during the design stage.

\section{Contribution and Thesis Structure}
\label{sec:chapter-one-contribution}

This thesis makes three primary contributions to the study of cryptocurrency manipulation. First, it establishes a formal link between social coordination signals and short-horizon market reactions, treating the message and the price response as parts of a single integrated process. Second, it shifts the focus from simple detection to the estimation of direct causal effects for specific signal characteristics. Finally, it explores how manipulation success is influenced by Market Capitalization and whether these events can be forecasted using information available at the time of the reveal.

Central to this work is the framing of coordinated messages as tools for coordination rather than sources of fundamental information. This perspective bridges the literature on financial manipulation with research on digital communication and high-frequency trading. It also provides a clear rationale for treating causal estimation and predictive modeling as distinct tasks.

The following chapters develop this argument in detail. Chapter~\ref{ch:data-description} focuses on data collection, event construction, and feature engineering. Chapter~\ref{ch:methodology} explains the formal causal framework, the heterogeneous-effect analysis, and the predictive design. Chapter~\ref{ch:results} then presents the empirical findings for each hypothesis. Finally, the appendix provides a detailed discussion of the synthetic-control experiments and explains their role in the broader analysis.

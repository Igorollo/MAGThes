This chapter reports the empirical results obtained from the methodology developed in Chapter 3. It first presents estimation diagnostics, then reports the main treatment effect estimates, heterogeneous-effect patterns, robustness checks, and predictive results used to assess the thesis hypotheses.

\section{Estimation Diagnostics}
\label{sec:sample-description}

Before estimation, multicollinearity among the three treatment variables was assessed using Variance Inflation Factors (VIF). All three variables remain well below the common rule-of-thumb threshold of 5 \cite{obrien2007}. Table~\ref{tab:vif-results} shows that Hype Intensity, Urgency Index, and Instruction Density all have VIF values below 1.2.

\begin{table}[htbp]

\begin{tabular}{lr}

\midrule
Hype Intensity & 1.189 \\
Urgency Index & 1.157 \\
Instruction Density & 1.126 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The DML estimator relies on nuisance models to residualize both the outcome and the treatment. The treatment model has a mean cross-validated $R^2$ of $-0.20$ with a range from $-0.29$ to $-0.06$, which indicates low predictive power of the observed confounders for treatment intensity in this sample rather than a close fit of the treatment model. The outcome model has a mean cross-validated $R^2$ of 0.39 for the 10-minute logarithmic return, which suggests moderate predictive power of the confounders for the outcome and supports the residualization step in the DML procedure. Taken together, these diagnostics indicate that the specification is estimable and that the residualization step captures useful outcome variation.

The stratified estimates show a clear market-cap gradient in treatment effects, especially for Urgency Index. The pattern is consistent with H2: the positive urgency effect is concentrated in lower-valued assets and weakens or turns negative as Market Capitalization increases.

In the small-cap stratum, the Urgency Index coefficient is 0.0301 (p $<$ 0.1\%), which indicates a positive relationship between higher message urgency and 10-minute log returns. This gives partial support for H1, mainly within the small-cap segment.

In the mid-cap stratum, the Urgency Index coefficient turns negative ($-$0.0081, p = 1.4\%). The positive urgency effect observed for small-cap assets therefore does not extend to larger market segments. Relative to H2, this estimate is consistent with weaker signal effects as Market Capitalization rises.

The negative coefficients in the large-cap stratum point in the same direction. The Urgency Index estimate of $-$0.0024 (p = 1.6\%) suggests that the urgency treatment effect is negative in the largest assets. This is also consistent with H2.

No treatment effect reaches conventional significance in the micro-cap stratum. Given the small sample size (N = 289) and the high degree of illiquidity in this group, H1 is not supported there. Taken together, the stratified estimates support H2 and provide partial support for H1, with the positive urgency effect concentrated in the small-cap segment rather than the full sample.

The sensitivity analysis therefore does not materially change the interpretation of the main small-cap urgency result.

\section{Predictive Modeling of Pump Success}
\label{sec:prediction-results}

This section reports the H3 predictive results for pump success, defined in Chapter~\ref{ch:methodology} as a positive 10-minute log return. After testing the candidate tree-based models described in Section~\ref{sec:predictive-modeling}, the XGBoost specification was selected as the best performing model and evaluated with the grouped holdout protocol. This unseen-channel evaluation is important because Figure~\ref{fig:channel-returns-distribution} shows that outcomes differ descriptively across channels.

Table~\ref{tab:classification-with-channel} presents the test set performance for this no-channel specification.

The strictly held-out model reaches a macro F1-score of 0.76, which indicates that pre-event market anomalies and signal characteristics alone contain highly meaningful predictive information. The model remarkably achieves 0.89 recall for pump successes, signaling it can identify the overwhelming majority of positive outcomes even when observing completely unseen channels. Consequently, the results provide direct support for H3 through asset and signal features rather than through a channel-history benchmark.

An additional evaluation was conducted to test whether formally including Channel ID could improve predictive performance. Such configuration didn't yield better results across test cases. However, even though channel names were removed, the data might still reflect the organizers' habits. Since different groups often follow specific patterns when picking coins or timing their signals, the model might naturally learn to recognize certain channels based on these unique signatures.

The threshold results show the expected precision-recall trade-off: higher thresholds improve precision at the cost of recall and overall trade frequency. Raising the threshold to 0.90 bolsters precision to 88.7\%, ensuring that almost 9 in 10 signals execute successfully while retaining a solid 256 predicted events on the held-out dataset. Overall, the rigorous grouped specification provides direct support for H3: observable pre-event market conditions and signal characteristics contain substantial predictive value even under strict out-of-sample evaluation limits.

To understand which features explicitly drive these predictive results, Figure~\ref{fig:feature-importance-no-channel} presents the top feature importances for the XGBoost model. The results show that a pump's success is mainly driven by the signal's characteristics, the age of the coin, and the market conditions at the time. Notably, \textit{instruction\_density} and \textit{hype\_volume} emerge as leading signal characteristics, highlighting the importance of the organizer's communication strategy. Temporal dynamics form another critical component, with the \textit{is\_weekend} feature demonstrating substantial predictive weight, suggesting that organizers may exploit structural shifts in market liquidity or retail attention during non-business days. Furthermore, the asset's overall maturity, captured by coin age, plays a significant role. Finally, the model heavily relies on immediate and baseline market thinness indicators. Both the target asset's prior illiquidity (\textit{pre\_amihud\_illiq}, \textit{pre\_amihud\_log\_30m}, short-run relative volume) and the comparative behavior of the synthetic control (\textit{pre\_zero\_ret\_ratio\_30m}, \textit{pre\_roll\_measure\_30m}, \textit{pre\_amihud\_illiq}) serve as strong predictors, confirming that for a pump to succeed, the coin's price must be easy to "move" or displace through coordinated buying. Although these variables are analyzed individually, they may all reflect a common underlying factor, such as the specific strategy or hidden profile of the organizing channel. Nevertheless, these findings confirm that observable market patterns and signal properties provide enough information to predict manipulation outcomes with significant accuracy, offering strong empirical support for H3.

The DML estimator relies on nuisance models to residualize both the outcome and the treatment. The treatment model has a mean cross-validated $R^2$ of $-0.12$, ranging from $-0.29$ to $-0.06$ across individual folds, which indicates low predictive power of the observed confounders for treatment intensity in this sample rather than a close fit of the treatment model. The outcome model has a mean cross-validated $R^2$ of 0.39 for the \textit{10-Minute Logarithmic Return}, which suggests moderate predictive power of the confounders for the outcome and supports the residualization step in the DML procedure. By residualizing the treatment as well, the estimator ensures that the resulting causal coefficients remain orthogonal to observed market factors, protecting the validity of the effect even when treatment intensity is difficult to predict. Taken together, these diagnostics indicate that the model setup is estimable and that the residualization step captures useful outcome variation.

The stratified estimates show that evidence differs across the three signal-characteristic variables rather than supporting a uniform positive effect. The clearest positive coefficient appears for \textit{Urgency Index} in the small-cap stratum, where the estimate is 0.0301 and is statistically significant at the 1\% level ($p < 0.001$). The corresponding \textit{Urgency Index} estimate turns negative and remains statistically significant at the 5\% level in the mid-cap stratum ($-$0.0081, $p = 0.014$) and the large-cap stratum ($-$0.0024, $p = 0.016$).

The micro-cap stratum has no treatment coefficient that reaches conventional significance. This stratum also has the smallest number of observations, 289, while the small- and mid-cap strata contain 1,182 and 967 observations, respectively. In the small-cap stratum, \textit{Hype Intensity} and \textit{Instruction Density} have weaker positive estimates that are only marginal at the 10\% level, while \textit{Urgency Index} is the only signal feature with clear conventional support in the expected positive direction. In the large-cap stratum, all three treatment variables have negative statistically significant estimates.

\datasetnote{CI E-value denotes the Confidence Interval E-value. M denotes million, B denotes billion.}

Approximate E-value diagnostics are reported as sensitivity checks for unmeasured confounding. Higher values suggest that a stronger unmeasured confounder would be needed to explain away the estimate, under the reported calculation. For the confidence-interval E-value, the value is set to 1 if the confidence interval crosses zero. Otherwise, the confidence bound closest to zero is used. Specifically, the E-value for the small-cap \textit{Urgency Index} effect (2.20) suggests that an unmeasured confounder would need a moderately strong association with both treatment and outcome to fully explain away the estimate. Conversely, the low E-values for large-cap \textit{Hype Intensity} and \textit{Instruction Density} indicate that those estimates are more sensitive to comparatively weak omitted confounding. Following \cite{vanderweele2017sensitivity}, this sensitivity result should be read as an approximate diagnostic rather than definitive proof of robustness.


\section{Predictive Modeling of Pump \textit{Success}}
\label{sec:prediction-results}

This section reports binary predictive results for pump outcomes, distinguishing \textit{Success} ($Y_{10}>0$) from \textit{Non-Success} ($Y_{10}\leq 0$), as defined in Chapter~\ref{ch:methodology}. After testing the candidate tree-based models described in Section~\ref{sec:predictive-modeling}, the XGBoost (Extreme Gradient Boosting) model was selected as the best performing model and evaluated with the grouped holdout protocol. The unseen-channel evaluation is important because Figure~\ref{fig:channel-returns-distribution} shows that outcomes differ descriptively across channels.

The strictly held-out model reaches a macro F1-score of 0.76 across the two outcome classes. The model achieves 0.89 recall for pump \textit{Success} cases, showing that it identifies many \textit{Success} cases even when observing completely unseen channels.

An additional evaluation was conducted to test whether formally including \textit{Channel ID} could improve predictive performance. That setup did not yield better results across test cases.

The threshold results show the expected precision-recall trade-off. Higher thresholds improve precision at the cost of recall and overall prediction frequency. Raising the threshold to 0.90 increases precision to 88.7\%, meaning that almost 9 in 10 predicted positive events are positive-return events in the held-out dataset, while retaining 256 predicted positive events.

The selected XGBoost model assigns aggregated relative importance across predictor groups. The leading groups include signal characteristics such as \textit{Hype Volume} and \textit{Instruction Density}, \textit{Coin Age}, realized-volatility measures, and market-thinness indicators such as Amihud illiquidity, zero-return ratios, and Roll spread estimates. This supports the predictive relevance of signal and pre-reveal market characteristics in the held-out classification task, but it does not imply that each predictor has a separate positive causal effect.

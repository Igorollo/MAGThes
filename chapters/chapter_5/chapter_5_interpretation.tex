% Section 4.3: Discussion
Building on the empirical results reported in Sections~\ref{sec:ate-results} and~\ref{sec:prediction-results}, this section discusses what the findings mean for the hypotheses and for the literature reviewed in Chapter~\ref{ch:literature-framework}. 

The results do not show that all message features consistently raise returns. Instead, they suggest that pump-and-dump signals depend strongly on context. The strongest positive evidence appears for the \textit{Urgency Index}, especially in a market range where assets are small enough to react to coordinated buying but active enough for stable estimation. The discussion below explains the behavioral mechanism behind urgent communication, the role of market size, and the relation between the causal estimates and the XGBoost predictive results.

The first hypothesis expected stronger signal characteristics to raise short-term post-reveal returns. Table~\ref{tab:stratified-ate-results} supports this hypothesis only in a narrow form. The clearest positive result is the small-cap estimate for \textit{Urgency Index}, with a coefficient of 0.0301 that is statistically significant at the 1\% level. Since the outcome is the \textit{10-Minute Logarithmic Return} and \textit{Urgency~Index} is scaled from 0 to 1, a 10 percentage point increase in the uppercase share of the signal corresponds to about 0.003 log-return units, or roughly a 0.30\% higher price return over ten minutes, holding the model's observed controls constant. This result is consistent with literature on public coordination, financial text and sentiment, and pump-message timing \cite{morris2002social, tetlock2007giving, xu2019anatomy, lamorgia2023doge}.

The stronger role of \textit{Urgency Index} is best understood as a timing result. In a pump group, the target reveal solves only part of the coordination problem. Members still need to believe that many others will react at almost the same time. Urgent wording helps create that belief by making delay feel costly and by turning the reveal into a short race for position. In this sense, urgency is not only an emotional style of writing. It is a device that compresses attention, expectation, and action into the first moments after $T_0$. This interpretation fits the public-signal logic discussed in Chapter~\ref{ch:literature-framework}: a public message can influence behavior when traders care about what others will do, not only about the asset's fundamental value \cite{morris2002social}. It also fits financial text research, where tone and wording can be linked to market activity even without new fundamental information \cite{tetlock2007giving}.

The results shown in the table do not support the first hypothesis as a general claim. In the micro-cap stratum, none of the three signal variables reaches conventional significance. In the mid-cap and large-cap strata, the \textit{Urgency Index} estimates are negative and statistically significant. In the large-cap stratum, all three signal variables are negative and statistically significant, although their sizes are small in practical terms. For example, a 10 percentage point increase in \textit{Urgency Index} implies about a 0.30\% higher return in the small-cap stratum, but only about a 0.02\% lower return in the large-cap stratum. The results therefore show a specific small-cap urgency pattern, not a general rule that stronger signals always raise prices.

This pattern fits the communication view from Chapter~\ref{ch:literature-framework}. Pre-reveal hype can gather attention, and instructions can reduce confusion, but urgency is closer to the moment when traders decide whether to act before others. In the small-cap stratum, \textit{Hype Intensity} and \textit{Instruction Density} show only marginal positive estimates, while \textit{Urgency Index} is the only signal feature with a clear positive effect at conventional levels. The finding therefore supports a narrower communication argument: in this sample, the reveal signal appears to matter most when it creates pressure to decide quickly \cite{barber2008all, tetlock2007giving, xu2019anatomy, lamorgia2023doge, hamrick2021examination}.

The weaker evidence for \textit{Hype Intensity} should not be read as evidence that pre-reveal communication is irrelevant. Hype takes place before the target coin is known, so it mainly prepares the audience rather than directing orders to a specific market. It can increase attention, remind members to be ready, and make the event feel shared. However, attention before the reveal can decay, be divided across other activities, or fail to turn into orders if the final signal is unclear. This helps explain why hype is theoretically important but less cleanly visible as a short-window return effect. Its role is closer to building a pool of waiting participants, while urgency is closer to activating that pool at the reveal moment.

\textit{Instruction Density} has a similarly conditional interpretation. In a fast cryptocurrency pump, longer instructions may reduce practical confusion when members need to identify the correct exchange, pair, or action within seconds. A message that gives enough detail can make it easier for attention to become actual order flow, especially for less experienced participants. At the same time, more words are not automatically better. A long signal may slow reading or create hesitation if the most important information is not immediately visible. For this reason, the marginal positive estimates for \textit{Instruction Density} in some strata are best read as consistent with an execution-clarity channel, not as proof that longer messages mechanically raise returns.

This leads directly to the second hypothesis, which receives the clearest but still qualified support. The stratified estimates indicate that \textit{Market Capitalization} changes the estimated effects of signal characteristics, with the clearest pattern appearing for \textit{Urgency Index}. The Causal~Forest pattern in Figure~\ref{fig:hte-market-cap} shows the same relationship for urgency specifically: the strongest positive urgency estimates appear around the lower market-cap range, roughly 7 million USD to 40 million USD. This is consistent with market-microstructure theory that order flow has a larger price impact in illiquid or low-depth markets \cite{amihud2002illiquidity, kyle1985continuous}, and with pump-and-dump evidence showing sharp short-run reactions after announcements \cite{li2021cryptocurrency, hamrick2021examination}.

The market-size pattern is not a simple ladder from smallest to largest assets. Chapter~\ref{ch:data-description} shows that coins below 10 million USD in \textit{Market Capitalization} often trade very sparsely, with over 68\% of the one-minute pre-pump intervals recording zero trading volume, and Table~\ref{tab:stratified-ate-results} reports only 289 micro-cap events. The lack of a significant micro-cap effect should therefore not be read as evidence that urgent signals never matter for the smallest assets. A more cautious interpretation is that the current sample cannot isolate a clear micro-cap urgency effect because these assets may be thin enough to move, but too inactive for stable estimation. In such markets, a small order can move the last traded price, but the observed return may also reflect stale prices, irregular execution, and very few actual trades. The same features that make micro-cap coins vulnerable to manipulation can therefore make the estimated effect unstable.

The more useful interpretation is that a pump target has to sit between two limits. On one side, a very deep market can absorb the coordinated buying wave with limited price impact. On the other side, a very inactive market may be easy to disturb but hard to measure reliably. The small-cap range appears closer to the middle of this trade-off. Such assets may still have enough activity for orders to be executed and prices to update, while remaining shallow enough for synchronized demand to matter. This connects the empirical pattern to the market-microstructure literature from Chapter~\ref{ch:literature-framework}, where price impact depends not only on the direction of order flow but also on the depth available to absorb it \cite{amihud2002illiquidity, kyle1985continuous}.

Larger-cap markets provide the other side of the same argument. They usually have broader participation, more resting liquidity, and more background trading activity. A short burst of demand from a Telegram group can be small relative to the normal flow of orders in such markets. Other traders may also supply liquidity or sell into the price movement more quickly. Under these conditions, urgent wording may still organize participants, but the market may not translate that coordination into a visible positive return at the same scale. This does not mean that communication loses all relevance in larger assets. It means that communication has to pass through a market structure that is harder to move.

Therefore, the mid-cap and large-cap estimates also require caution. Their negative signs, together with the small practical size of the large-cap estimates, are consistent with the idea that urgent wording loses force when markets become deeper, but they do not prove that urgency mechanically lowers returns in larger assets. Several interpretations are possible. Organizers may use more forceful language when they know that the target is harder to move, so urgency may partly reflect compensation for a difficult setup. Some larger-cap targets may also be chosen for visibility or credibility rather than pure price impact. In addition, traders may read very aggressive wording differently in a more liquid market, especially if they believe that many others are trying to exit quickly. The DML model adjusts for observed pre-reveal conditions, but it cannot remove every possible unobserved difference between events. The stronger conclusion is that the expected lower-cap gradient is qualified: the detectable positive urgency effect appears in a market range that is thin enough to react but active enough to measure.

The predictive results add a different perspective and speak directly to the third hypothesis. The \textit{Channel ID}-agnostic XGBoost model in Table~\ref{tab:classification-no-channel} reaches a macro F1-score of about 0.76 and a recall of about 0.89 for \textit{Success} cases on a grouped holdout test set with unseen channels. This supports the third hypothesis as a forecasting claim. It shows that pre-event market variables and signal characteristics contain information that helps classify pump events as either \textit{Success} or \textit{Non-Success}, based on whether the \textit{10-Minute Logarithmic Return} is positive. This finding is related to the detection and target-prediction literature reviewed in Chapter~\ref{ch:literature-framework}, which shows that pump events leave observable traces in market and communication data \cite{kamps2018pump, victor2019cryptocurrency, lamorgia2020pump, hu2023sequence}.

However, the predictive model should not be interpreted as a causal model. Feature importance in Figure~\ref{fig:feature-importance-xgboost-top10} ranks variables that help XGBoost separate \textit{Success} from \textit{Non-Success} events, but it does not show which variables independently caused the return. The leading predictors include signal features such as \textit{Hype Volume} and \textit{Instruction Density}, together with \textit{Coin Age}, realized-volatility measures, Amihud illiquidity measures, Roll spread estimates, and zero-return measures. These variables may predict the binary outcome label because they describe the message, target asset, target-market state, and synthetic-control comparison, not because each variable has a separate causal effect on returns. This is why the causal and predictive parts should be read together but not treated as the same result, following the broader distinction between explanation and prediction \cite{shmueli2010explain}. The causal model measures the direct impact of urgency after adjusting for other variables. The predictive model asks whether the available pre-event information can classify outcomes.

The value of the predictive result is therefore different from the value of the DML estimates. Good classification on held-out channels suggests that pump outcomes are not just random reactions after the signal. They contain structure that is visible before or at the reveal, including the condition of the target market and the design of the communication. This supports the broader argument of the thesis, since a purely random short-window reaction would be difficult to forecast from pre-event variables. At the same time, predictive structure can arise from many sources. A model may learn that certain combinations of illiquidity, volatility, coin age, hype, and instructions often appear before positive outcomes, without knowing which element actually caused the return. For this reason, prediction strengthens the claim that pump events are organized and patterned, but it does not replace causal interpretation.

The two parts still support one broader interpretation. Pump outcomes are not random reactions to isolated messages. They are structured by the interaction between message design, asset characteristics, timing, and pre-reveal market conditions. The causal model focuses on a smaller set of theoretically motivated signal features, while the predictive model allows many variables to work together. 

The threshold analysis in Table~\ref{tab:threshold-optimization} gives the predictive result a practical meaning, but also shows why it should not be overstated. Raising the decision threshold increases precision and reduces the number of predicted positive events, while recall falls. This trade-off is useful for understanding model behavior, but it is not the same as a trading rule. In this thesis, \textit{Success} means only that the \textit{10-Minute Logarithmic Return} is positive. It does not mean that a trader could enter and exit at the right moments, avoid slippage, pay fees, and still realize a profit. The fixed 10-minute checkpoint is a standardized way to compare events, not a full measure of event profitability, which follows the event-window logic discussed in Chapter~\ref{ch:literature-framework} \cite{li2021cryptocurrency, mackinlay1997event}.

Several limits remain important for both the causal and predictive findings. The study is observational, and pump signals were not randomly assigned. Organizers may vary hype, urgency, or instruction density when they already expect a coin to move, or when they believe participants need stronger pressure. This means that signal style can reflect organizer expectations as well as influence participant behavior. The DML design reduces this problem by adjusting for observed pre-reveal conditions, but it cannot fully separate every strategic choice made before the signal. The E-value results in Table~\ref{tab:evalue-results} provide approximate sensitivity diagnostics for unmeasured confounding under the reported calculation \cite{vanderweele2017sensitivity}, but they do not remove this concern. The reported E-value only suggests that the small-cap urgency result would require a stronger unmeasured confounder to explain it away than some other significant estimates.

The use of \textit{Market Capitalization} also limits the interpretation. The thesis treats market capitalization as a practical proxy for market depth and vulnerability, not as a direct measure of order-book liquidity. This matters because two coins with similar capitalization can still differ in trading depth, exchange structure, spread, and active market participation. The result therefore supports a market-cap boundary condition, but future work with richer order-book data could test the same mechanism more directly.

Finally, the grouped holdout design reduces direct dependence on \textit{Channel ID}, but it cannot fully remove organizer patterns. Figure~\ref{fig:channel-returns-distribution} shows that outcomes differ across Telegram groups, even though many large channels remain close to zero in median return. Channels may repeatedly choose similar coins, exchanges, timings, or message styles, and these habits can still appear indirectly through other variables. This limitation is consistent with the literature on organized pump groups and target prediction, where channel behavior, message structure, and pre-event traces are part of the same environment \cite{hamrick2021examination, xu2019anatomy, lamorgia2023doge, hu2023sequence}. This limitation should be kept in mind when reading the predictive results, because the grouped holdout design tests generalization across observed channels rather than across all possible pump settings.

Overall, the discussion points to a joint communication and market-structure interpretation. Pump messages matter because they help turn dispersed attention into coordinated action at a precise moment. Urgent wording is especially important because it addresses the timing problem at the reveal, while hype and instructions support the surrounding process of attention and execution. Yet communication alone is not enough. The same message can have different effects depending on whether the target market has enough depth to absorb orders or enough thinness for orders to move prices. The most defensible conclusion is therefore not that stronger messages always create higher returns, but that pump communication works within market constraints. The public signal organizes traders, while liquidity, activity, and target selection determine how much of that organization becomes an observable price reaction.

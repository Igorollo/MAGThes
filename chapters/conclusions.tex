This thesis examined whether the wording and timing of Telegram pump-and-dump signals help explain the immediate price reaction after the target coin is revealed. The answer is conditional. The public signal can matter, especially when urgency appears in a market where coordinated demand can still move the price. The results do not support a general claim that stronger hype, urgency, or instructions always produce higher returns. The effect depends on communication design and market conditions.

The research problem was not whether cryptocurrency pump-and-dump schemes exist. Earlier studies already show that they can be detected and linked to short-term price and volume changes \cite{kamps2018pump, xu2019anatomy, hamrick2021examination, lamorgia2020pump, li2021cryptocurrency}. The more specific question was whether the public signal itself helps explain the immediate market response. This matters because a pump message is not ordinary financial news. It is a coordination tool that tries to make many traders act at the same moment \cite{morris2002social, xu2019anatomy, lamorgia2023doge}. Understanding this communication layer is important for market-manipulation research, investor protection, and the analysis of thin cryptocurrency markets \cite{amihud2002illiquidity, hamrick2021examination, charfeddine2024drives}.

The study was guided by three hypotheses. Stronger values of \textit{Hype Intensity}, \textit{Urgency Index}, and \textit{Instruction Density} were hypothesized to have positive estimated effects on short-term cryptocurrency returns after the coin reveal. These effects were also hypothesized to be stronger in lower-cap assets than in higher-cap assets, and to weaken as \textit{Market Capitalization} increased. Finally, observable market patterns up to the reveal, combined with the same signal characteristics, were hypothesized to contain information that could help classify the binary \textit{Success}/\textit{Non-Success} outcome.

The empirical analysis used a pump-event dataset combining three sources of information. Telegram messages identified pump sessions and signal characteristics around the reveal. One-minute OHLCV records measured the short-window market response and pre-reveal market conditions. Historical CoinMarketCap metadata supplied asset-level information, including \textit{Coin Age}, \textit{Market Capitalization}, and trading-volume measures. After validation and complete-case restrictions, the final causal sample contained 2,917 pump events.

The methodology separated causal explanation from forecasting. The causal part used DML (Double Machine Learning) to estimate the relationship between signal characteristics and the \textit{10-Minute Logarithmic Return}, while adjusting for pre-reveal market conditions, asset characteristics, and channel differences. It also compared effects across \textit{Market Capitalization} strata and used heterogeneous-effect analysis to examine changes with market size. The predictive part used tree-based machine learning, mainly a \textit{Channel ID}-agnostic XGBoost model, to classify positive and non-positive short-window outcomes.

The first hypothesis, that stronger signal characteristics would generally increase short-term returns, receives only partial support. \textit{Urgency Index} has the clearest positive estimated role in the small-cap segment, while \textit{Hype Intensity} and \textit{Instruction Density} show weaker and less stable patterns. A more careful conclusion is that urgency can matter when the target market is small enough for coordinated demand to have an effect.

The second hypothesis, that signal effects would be stronger in lower-cap assets, receives clearer but still qualified support. Market size changes the estimated effects of signal characteristics, especially \textit{Urgency Index}. The evidence does not show that the smallest assets are always the easiest to move, since micro-cap trading is often sparse and hard to estimate. The stronger conclusion is that signal characteristics matter most when the target market is thin enough to react, but still active enough for measurement.

The third hypothesis, that pre-reveal information could help classify pump outcomes, is supported as a forecasting result. The \textit{Channel ID}-agnostic XGBoost model shows useful holdout performance on unseen channels. This result remains separate from the causal findings because prediction answers a different question from causal explanation. A variable can help classify outcomes without being the direct cause of the return.

The thesis contributes to the study of cryptocurrency manipulation by treating pump messages as part of the manipulation mechanism, not only as labels for abnormal price movements. The public message is the moment where coordination becomes visible. Overall, the findings show that the social design of the signal helps organize traders at the reveal, while market conditions shape whether coordinated demand can move the price.

These conclusions should be read within the limits of the research design. The study is observational, so unmeasured confounding cannot be fully ruled out. DML adjusts for available pre-reveal information, but it cannot make the signals random. The \textit{10-Minute Logarithmic Return} captures the immediate market reaction, not full event profitability with entry delays, fees, or slippage. \textit{Market Capitalization} is a useful proxy for market depth, but it is not direct order-book liquidity. Channel or organizer patterns may also affect both signal style and outcomes.

Future research could test the same mechanism with richer order-book data, execution and slippage information, longer post-event windows, more recent market periods, and broader market coverage. Order-book data would measure market depth more directly than market capitalization. Execution and slippage data would help distinguish standardized outcomes from realistic trading profitability. Broader samples could test whether the same communication patterns remain visible as cryptocurrency markets, exchanges, and messaging platforms change.

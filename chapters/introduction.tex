\chapter*{INTRODUCTION}
\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Introduction}

Cryptocurrencies were originally developed to make digital money transfers possible without the involvement of banks or central authorities. The main idea was to create a system in which users could send and receive value directly, without relying on a trusted third party to process or verify transactions. This move toward decentralization replaced traditional institutional trust with a system in which transactions are governed by code and recorded on a shared digital network.

Today, however, the cryptocurrency market has developed into a much more complex system. While many projects still aim to build useful financial infrastructure, a large part of the market is driven by social media attention and speculative trading. New assets are often launched and traded globally before their actual use can be properly assessed. For some, this volatility creates opportunities, but it also opens the door to organized manipulation that targets less experienced investors.

This thesis examines pump-and-dump schemes coordinated through messaging platforms, where a target asset is announced to a group of traders at the same time.
The goal is to determine whether the characteristics of these signals help explain the immediate market response. The analysis also looks at how this response may differ depending on the size and liquidity of the targeted asset. This distinction is important because smaller, less active markets often react much more strongly to organized signals than larger, more liquid ones.

Besides explaining these price movements, the thesis also includes a predictive part. It investigates whether the information available at the time of a signal can help identify which events are likely to lead to positive short-term returns. In this way, the thesis separates the task of explaining the market's reaction from the task of predicting which events will be successful.
